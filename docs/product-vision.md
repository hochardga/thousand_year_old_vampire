# Product Vision — Thousand Year Old Vampire: Digital Edition

## 1. Vision & Mission
### Vision Statement
Reflective players who would never begin a paper journaling game can still enter a centuries-long gothic life, lose themselves in it, and come away with a chronicle that feels intimate, unsettling, and wholly their own.

### Mission Statement
Build a web-first digital edition of *Thousand Year Old Vampire* that removes setup and bookkeeping friction while preserving the literary solitude, memory erosion, and ritual weight of the original experience.

### Founder's Why
Gerg is not approaching this as a hobbyist tinkering with a niche rules set. He is bringing more than twenty years of experience building enterprise software to a very different kind of problem: how to reduce friction in a dense system without stripping away what gives it meaning. That is unusually relevant here. The digital adaptation challenge is not “make the rules easier.” It is “make the rules recede at the right moment so the fiction can arrive sooner.”

He also has a sharper personal reason than “I like the game.” This is his first attempt to release something creative that feels authored rather than merely useful. That matters because it creates the right kind of pressure. The product cannot hide behind technical cleverness or feature sprawl; it has to feel emotionally durable, atmospheric, and worthy of being pointed to as a finished creative work.

That combination is the founder-market fit: a builder who knows how to tame complexity, but who is motivated to use that discipline in service of tone, pacing, and literary feeling rather than admin efficiency. If the product goes wrong, it will likely go wrong by becoming too system-forward. Gerg is unusually equipped to catch that failure mode early.

### Core Values
- **Reduce friction, never depth.** Every simplification must protect the emotional payload of the game. If a change makes the product easier to use but less haunting, it is the wrong change.
- **The player's words are the artifact.** The system may structure, preserve, and reveal consequences, but it must not become the author. The player's writing remains the heart of the experience.
- **Ritual beats interface.** Whenever there is a tension between conventional app affordances and a calmer, more immersive flow, choose the path that keeps the player in the fiction, provided clarity does not break.
- **Finish the narrow version.** This project succeeds by releasing a sharply scoped, authored digital edition, not by aspiring to become a general story platform. Scope discipline is part of the product quality.
- **Private by default.** This is a solitary game about interiority and memory. Accounts, archives, and product choices should make the player feel held and protected, not exposed or pushed toward performance.

### Strategic Pillars
- **Enter the fiction fast.** The first session has one job: move the player from curiosity to inhabiting a vampire's life within minutes. Anything that slows that journey must be questioned.
- **Make the archive meaningful.** The chronicle, memory ledger, and diary are not support views; they are core product surfaces where the theme of identity erosion becomes tangible.
- **Guide constraint in context.** The product should lead players through legal and meaningful choices exactly when those choices matter, instead of making them manage rules through visible system controls.
- **Build one excellent edition, not a platform.** The roadmap should privilege faithfulness, return-session continuity, and polish over extensibility, social features, or content authoring tools.

### Success Looks Like
Twelve months from now, the product is known inside narrative game circles as the cleanest and most welcoming way to enter *Thousand Year Old Vampire* without losing its literary soul. The public version has 800 registered players, at least 250 of them have started chronicles, and 75 have returned for three or more sessions. A few respected designers, reviewers, or community organizers have pointed to it as a thoughtful adaptation rather than a gimmick. Just as importantly, Gerg can point to it as a finished creative work with a clear point of view. The biggest qualifier is legal: this version of success assumes the project has clarified its licensing posture before full public distribution.

## 2. User Research
### Primary Persona
Jules is 35, digitally fluent, creatively curious, and drawn to solitary experiences that feel intimate rather than performative. They read literary fiction, occasionally play visual novels or choice-driven indies, and like the idea of journaling games more than their own history of actually starting them. Their day is fragmented and screen-heavy, which means any experience that asks them to assemble paper tools, track state manually, or write into a blank page without guidance feels like work before it feels transporting. They are comfortable with well-designed web apps, mildly allergic to clutter, and highly sensitive to tone. What would make them switch is not more features; it is the feeling that this product understands how to guide them into the story without flattening it into content consumption.

### Secondary Personas
Rowan is an existing fan of the tabletop game who wants a faithful digital edition for convenience, portability, and easier rereading. Rowan is not the core acquisition target, but they are a crucial legitimacy audience because they will immediately notice whether the adaptation respects the source material.

Avery is adjacent to the genre rather than already inside it. They enjoy reflective digital experiences, horror-flavored narrative design, and authored software objects, but they do not identify as a tabletop player. Avery matters because they represent expansion beyond the current fan base if the onboarding is strong enough.

Lena is a reviewer, designer, or community organizer in the solo or story-game space. She may not become a heavy long-term user herself, but she can validate whether the adaptation feels thoughtful and worth recommending. She is a force multiplier, not the primary customer.

### Jobs To Be Done
Functionally, the user hires this product to let them start and sustain a *Thousand Year Old Vampire* chronicle without wrestling paper state management, prompt lookups, or manual memory bookkeeping. They want the game to move with them across devices and across time gaps.

Emotionally, they hire it to feel drawn inward quickly. They want the product to create a sense of private ritual, not an atmosphere of task completion. They want to feel guided, not supervised; immersed, not instructed.

Socially, even though the experience is solitary, users still want the product to support a kind of self-perception: “I am someone who actually finishes thoughtful, literary play experiences.” Secondary users such as fans and critics also want to feel they have found a version of the game that respects the original instead of commodifying it.

### Pain Points
The most severe pain point is **startup friction**. The user is intrigued by the experience, but handwriting, setup, and bookkeeping feel like homework before the fiction arrives. This happens at the exact moment when curiosity is most fragile, so the consequence is abandonment before the game can prove itself.

The second pain point is **continuity burden**. Even players who begin can lose momentum because memories, characters, losses, and prompt movement become hard to track across sessions. The consequence is not merely confusion; it is the collapse of mood, because uncertainty about state displaces the feeling of living through a cursed life.

The third pain point is **tool mismatch**. Plain documents, note apps, and DIY templates can store text, but they cannot transform writing into a felt archive of memory and erosion. The user is left with text capture but not shaped experience.

The fourth pain point is **genre intimidation**. For adjacent users, “solo journaling game” can sound like a niche ritual requiring specialized patience or analog enthusiasm. The pain is mostly emotional rather than operational, but it still meaningfully reduces adoption.

### Current Alternatives & Competitive Landscape
The strongest alternative is still the original physical book and handwritten journaling. It does the atmosphere well because it is the source material, and for committed analog players it likely remains the preferred form. But for the target audience here, it demands too much friction up front and too much manual continuity later.

The next alternative is a stack of generic digital tools such as Notes, Obsidian, Notion, or plain markdown documents. These are flexible and familiar, which is why people reach for them, but they flatten the ritual into document management. They preserve text while losing structure, pacing, and the mechanical pressure of memory and forgetting.

Adjacent digital story games, visual novels, and journaling apps form a looser competitive set. They often do onboarding and aesthetics better, but they are not really solving the same job. They do not deliver the specific identity erosion, prompt motion, and archive logic that define this game.

“Do nothing” is a meaningful competitor. Many people simply admire journaling games from a distance and never begin. That inertia is part of the market reality, not just a side condition.

### Key Assumptions to Validate
- We assume players who bounce off analog play still want the emotional intensity of the source material, not a softened cousin of it. To validate: interview new players after their first session and ask whether the product felt welcoming without losing its edge.
- We assume the fastest path to value is a guided setup plus early prompt flow rather than a broader sandbox of options. To validate: measure completion rate from sign-up to first resolved prompt and compare it against drop-off during setup.
- We assume the archive can become a differentiating surface rather than a secondary utility view. To validate: track whether users revisit archive and recap screens between active sessions.
- We assume account creation will not destroy the magic moment if the auth flow is quiet enough. To validate: instrument sign-up abandonment and test whether delaying account commitment improves early completion.
- We assume existing fans will tolerate automation as long as authorship remains manual. To validate: recruit tabletop fans into beta and specifically ask where the product crosses the line into over-scripting.
- We assume a web-first responsive product is enough for the initial audience. To validate: track device mix and measure whether mobile players complete sessions at materially lower rates.
- We assume build-in-public content about craft and tone will attract the right users more effectively than generic indie-dev posting. To validate: observe which content themes generate signups from the target communities.
- We assume public launch is legally viable, or that a narrower beta can proceed while licensing is clarified. To validate: resolve the adaptation rights question before any wide release.

### User Journey Map
Awareness begins when Jules sees a screenshot, short devlog, or recommendation from a narrative-game community. What catches them is not “this automates journaling” but “this looks like a private object I want to spend time with.” Consideration is brief but fragile: they scan for whether the product looks tasteful, whether it feels like a real adaptation instead of a themed wrapper, and whether getting started seems lightweight enough to fit a tired evening.

First use has to avoid the blank-page problem. The player should feel ushered into becoming a vampire before they are asked to internalize the whole rules system. The mood at this stage is cautious curiosity. If setup turns procedural, they are gone.

The magic moment arrives when the player realizes they are already inside a life rather than learning software. That happens when their first setup choices and early writing are reflected back as an evolving chronicle. Habit formation then depends on the Return Layer: recaps, resumable sessions, and an archive worth reopening. Advocacy is likely to be quiet but meaningful: a recommendation in a Discord thread, a screenshot shared with affection, a comment that “this actually preserves the feeling.”

## 3. Product Strategy
### Product Principles
- **The first session must feel authored.** Even before the product is feature-complete, the opening journey must feel deliberate and polished enough to justify trust.
- **Keep the player in one emotional lane at a time.** Active play is for prompt, writing, and immediate consequence; the archive is where the broader structure becomes legible.
- **Visible state should read as narrative consequence.** Memory loss, diary storage, and trait changes should feel like events in the chronicle, not CRUD updates.
- **Use automation to remove burden, not decision-making.** The product should never replace the player's interpretive work or prose voice.
- **Mobile is a valid play surface, not a fallback.** Cross-device continuity is part of the promise, so responsive behavior must be designed rather than bolted on.
- **Every addition must earn its place by strengthening the magic moment or return-session continuity.** Nice ideas that do neither belong later.

### Market Differentiation
The differentiation is not “digital version of a tabletop game.” That is too shallow and too easy to replicate badly. The product is differentiated because it is designed around a specific tension: many people want the depth and atmosphere of solo journaling play, but the manual apparatus prevents them from ever reaching it. Generic note tools solve storage, not ritual. Broader narrative apps solve convenience, not literary intimacy. This product is defensible if it becomes known for honoring the source material while making the first and fifth sessions equally accessible. That combination of fidelity, restraint, and continuity is harder than it looks, which is exactly why it can matter.

### Magic Moment Design
The magic moment is the player's transition from “I am learning a complicated solo game” to “I am already inside a vampire's life.” For that to happen reliably, three things must be true. First, setup has to be scaffolded into meaningful creative steps rather than rules explanation. Second, the first active prompt must arrive quickly, with the interface already carrying enough atmosphere that the user feels the shift. Third, the archive must immediately reflect what the player wrote so the fiction feels preserved, not transient.

The shortest path to this moment is: arrive, create or open a chronicle, complete guided becoming-undead setup, answer the first prompt, and see the chronicle update around that answer. If the player must navigate separate dashboards, settings, or explanatory screens before that chain completes, the product is off course.

### MVP Definition
The MVP must include guided chronicle creation, a focused setup flow, prompt-by-prompt play, automatic movement and rule handling, durable state for memories and traits, diary logic, session recaps, and a rereadable archive. Each of these is essential because the product promise is not only “start the game” but “sustain a haunting continuity over time.”

Done for guided setup means a new player can create a chronicle, establish mortal life, define key relationships and curse details, and reach the first active prompt without consulting external rules. Done for prompt play means the system can present the current prompt, accept writing, resolve rolls, apply state changes, and move to the next position. Done for the archive means the player can review memories, characters, resources, skills, marks, and session history in a way that is legible and atmospheric. Done for return-session support means a player can stop, come back days later, and understand where they are emotionally and mechanically.

### Explicitly Out of Scope
Multiplayer, comments, community discovery, and social sharing are out of scope because they contradict both the solitary tone and the need for a narrow first release. Heavy customization such as alternate themes, prompt packs, or homebrew tools is also out of scope because it turns a faithful edition into a platform project. AI-written prose assistance is out because it compromises authorship at the exact place the product must be disciplined. Native shells for iOS, Android, or desktop are deferred because the responsive web product already covers the core use case. Monetization is out because the project is explicitly free for now, and billing work would distract from proving the creative core.

The tempting feature to watch most carefully is export and public sharing. It sounds useful, and eventually it may be, but it can wait until the archive itself is excellent. Revisit it only after the core return-session loop is stable.

### Feature Priority (MoSCoW)
**Must Have:** guided setup; prompt presentation and resolution; automatic dice and movement handling; durable chronicle state; memory, diary, and trait tracking; session recap; responsive archive view; private account-based persistence.

**Should Have:** atmospheric landing page; thoughtful empty states; timeline-style recap; mobile-optimized writing flow; analytics on first-session completion and return usage.

**Could Have:** read-only export; sample chronicle; optional local draft before sign-in; richer ornamental archive polish; beta feedback inbox inside the product.

**Won't Have (this time):** multiplayer, community gallery, monetization, custom prompt authoring, AI co-writing, native mobile packaging, and achievement systems.

### Core User Flows
The first critical flow is **Start a Chronicle**. Trigger: the user clicks start from the landing page. Steps: create account or restore session, begin guided setup, define mortal identity and initial traits, become undead, enter the first prompt. Outcome: the player feels they are already inhabiting a life. Success criteria: the majority of successful signups reach the first prompt in one sitting.

The second critical flow is **Resolve a Prompt and Update the Chronicle**. Trigger: the player completes a writing response. Steps: submit response, resolve dice and movement, apply memory or trait changes, record the experience, show the next prompt position, surface a brief archive consequence. Outcome: the player sees the system working for them without stealing narrative control. Success criteria: responses save reliably, updates feel immediate, and state remains coherent.

The third critical flow is **Return After a Gap**. Trigger: the player comes back after hours or days away. Steps: open the chronicle, read a short recap, review recent archive changes if needed, resume active play. Outcome: the player regains emotional and mechanical context quickly. Success criteria: return users do not bounce because they feel lost.

### Success Metrics
The primary metric is **returning chronicles**: at least 20 unique players create a chronicle and at least 8 of them return for a second session within 14 days. Good means the product is more than a curiosity. Great means 15 or more return within that window.

The most important activation metric is **time to first resolved prompt**. Good is under 10 minutes from sign-up for a new user; great is under 6 minutes without harming tone. A supporting quality metric is **setup completion rate**. Good is 50 percent of users who start setup reaching the first prompt; great is 70 percent.

The key retention leading indicator is **archive revisit rate**. Good is 40 percent of return users opening archive or recap before resuming play; great is 60 percent, because that suggests the archive is becoming part of the ritual rather than leftover state.

### Risks
The highest-risk issue is **licensing uncertainty**. If the project lacks rights for public digital distribution of the game's content, launch options narrow immediately. Mitigation: resolve the rights question early and treat public release scope as contingent until that is clear.

The next risk is **over-engineering**. The founder's technical strength can become a trap if the project expands into generalized systems instead of a tight edition. Mitigation: preserve a ruthless MVP and use the roadmap as a scope boundary.

There is also a **tone failure** risk. A product can technically implement the rules and still feel like admin software. Mitigation: build tone checks into design reviews and user testing, especially around setup, archive, and error states.

Another risk is **auth friction**. If account creation feels louder than the fiction, users may churn before the magic moment. Mitigation: use a quiet auth flow and test whether early draft state should be allowed pre-sign-in.

The product also carries a **mobile writing risk**. If the responsive experience collapses into cramped panels or keyboard battles, cross-device credibility suffers. Mitigation: design the writing surface mobile-first, not desktop-first.

Finally, there is **audience mismatch** risk. Build-in-public attracts many developers, but not all of them are the intended players. Mitigation: post about ritual, craft, atmosphere, and adaptation decisions, not generic indie-dev progress.

## 4. Brand Strategy
### Positioning Statement
For reflective players who want intimate narrative play but bounce off analog friction, *Thousand Year Old Vampire: Digital Edition* is the guided digital edition that lets them enter and sustain a centuries-long gothic chronicle without losing the literary soul of the original. Unlike handwritten play, generic note apps, or broader narrative game apps, it preserves solitude, memory erosion, and authorship while quietly carrying the rules burden underneath.

### Brand Personality
This product is a graceful guide through darkness. If it were a person, it would speak softly, dress with restraint, and know exactly how to invite someone into a serious conversation without becoming stern or melodramatic. It is well-read, attentive, and emotionally composed. It never winks at the user, never over-performs its gothic influences, and never behaves like an eager productivity assistant wearing dark lipstick.

### Voice & Tone Guide
The voice stays constant: calm, intimate, literate, and quietly supportive. Tone changes by context, but it never becomes perkily helpful, gamified, or faux-mystical.

| Context | Do | Don't |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Onboarding | “We will begin with the life you had before.” | “Let’s get you set up in just a few easy steps!” |
| Error states | “That memory could not be saved. Try again when you are ready.” | “Oops! Something went wrong.” |
| Empty states | “No diary has been kept yet. What is forgotten still lives only in the mind.” | “You haven’t added any items yet.” |
| Success messages | “The entry has been set into memory.” | “Success! Your changes were saved.” |
| Marketing copy | “A quiet digital ritual for living through centuries of memory and loss.” | “The easiest way to manage your vampire campaign.” |

### Messaging Framework
The tagline should be: **A guided digital ritual for a solitary gothic life.**

The homepage headline should be: **Enter the vampire's life before the rules get in the way.** The subhead should clarify that this is a faithful digital edition designed for players who want the literary depth of the original without the analog burden.

Three core value propositions matter. First, **start quickly without losing the mood**: guided setup gets players into the fiction within minutes. Second, **let the archive carry the centuries**: the product remembers what matters so the player can keep writing. Third, **preserve authorship**: the app structures and protects the chronicle without writing it for the player.

Feature descriptions should stay grounded in feeling. Guided setup is not a wizard; it is a becoming-undead sequence. Archive is not a dashboard; it is a living ledger. Session recap is not state restore; it is return to the night you left.

The most likely objection is that digital adaptation will make the game feel too streamlined. The response is: this edition does not simplify the soul of the game; it simplifies the burden around it. The second objection is that solo journaling sounds intimidating. The response is: this version exists precisely to turn curiosity into an actual first session.

### Elevator Pitches
**5-second:** A digital edition of *Thousand Year Old Vampire* that gets you into the fiction without the bookkeeping.

**30-second:** It is a web-first adaptation of the solitary gothic journaling game *Thousand Year Old Vampire*. The product guides you through becoming undead, handles the rules and memory tracking quietly in the background, and turns your writing into a living archive you can return to over time. The goal is not to gamify it; the goal is to preserve the literary mood while removing the friction that keeps many people from ever starting.

**2-minute:** A lot of people are drawn to solo journaling games because they promise something most games don't: intimacy, reflection, and a sense of authorship. But many of those same people never begin, because handwriting, setup, and manual bookkeeping feel like work before the magic arrives. *Thousand Year Old Vampire* is a beautiful example of that tension. The original game is powerful, but it asks a lot from players who are already living on screens. This product is a digital edition built specifically to solve that problem. It guides you through your mortal life and your curse, presents each prompt as a dramatic beat, quietly handles rolls and state transitions, and preserves everything inside an archive that makes memory loss and reinvention feel tangible. The player still writes the story. The app just removes the scaffolding burden so the experience can actually happen.

### Competitive Differentiation Narrative
Competitors and alternatives break down along the wrong axis. The physical book has the soul but asks for analog commitment. Note apps have convenience but no ritual structure. Broader narrative game apps make interaction legible but often sand away the literary solitude that gives this game its bite. This product's advantage is that it is built around the tension itself. It knows the user wants to be guided, but not managed; supported, but not entertained at. That makes it a better fit for curious newcomers and a more interesting adaptation for existing fans.

### Brand Anti-Patterns
Never let the product feel like a dense rules tool. That means no spreadsheet layouts, no form-heavy setup that reads like admin intake, and no interfaces that foreground system state over emotional context.

Never let it feel like horror wallpaper on generic software. Avoid excessive ornament, muddy faux-gothic clutter, obvious trope imagery, or copy that tries too hard to sound ominous.

Never let it sound like productivity software. Avoid bright encouragement, achievement framing, and transactional UI language such as “manage,” “optimize,” or “track your progress.” The user is not operating a workflow; they are inhabiting a cursed life.

## 5. Design Direction
### Design Philosophy
The design should behave like a quiet nocturne rather than a theatrical stage set. It should use restraint to create atmosphere, letting typography, spacing, and tonal contrast do most of the work. The primary rule is that the writing surface must feel protected. The second rule is that the archive should feel discovered, not administered. The third is that every state transition should preserve legibility even when it carries emotional weight.

### Visual Mood
The visual reference point is not “gothic UI” in the Halloween sense. It is closer to a late-night reading room: deep charcoal around warm paper surfaces, faded gold used sparingly, and enough spaciousness that each prompt or memory can feel held. Think editorial quiet, not baroque clutter. The mood should feel literary and nocturnal without sinking readability into darkness.

Light mode is the primary mode for launch because long-form writing and rereading benefit from warm paper surfaces. If dark mode is added later, it should invert shell and surface roles without sacrificing text contrast or turning the archive into a neon goth theme.

### Typography
Use **Cormorant Garamond** for headings, loaded in weights 500, 600, and 700 with italics available for title moments. Use **Newsreader** for body copy, loaded in weights 400, 500, and 600. Use **IBM Plex Mono** for dates, prompt indexes, archival metadata, and system labels.

Define typography tokens as follows: `--font-heading: "Cormorant Garamond", serif;`, `--font-body: "Newsreader", serif;`, and `--font-mono: "IBM Plex Mono", monospace;`. Size scale: `0.875rem`, `1rem`, `1.125rem`, `1.375rem`, `1.75rem`, `2.25rem`, `3rem`. Line heights: 1.6 for body text, 1.3 for section headings, 1.1 for display titles. Keep the writing area at 65–72 characters per line on large screens.

### Spacing & Layout
Use a 4px spacing grid with tokens at `4`, `8`, `12`, `16`, `24`, `32`, `48`, `64`, and `96`. Minimum vertical spacing between stacked sections is 24px on mobile and 32px on tablet/desktop. Max reading width for prompt and writing surfaces is 48rem. The overall shell should cap at 72rem with generous outer gutters: 16px on mobile, 24px on tablet, and 40px on desktop. Use a two-region layout on large screens only when it genuinely supports focus; archive and ledger content should collapse into stacked panels on smaller screens rather than shrinking into dense columns.

### Component Philosophy
Components should feel like restrained editorial artifacts. Prefer hairline borders or tonal shifts over heavy cards. Use 4px radius on interactive controls and 0–2px radius on document-like panels. Shadows should be soft and infrequent: a low-contrast diffuse shadow for floating layers, none for standard surfaces. Primary buttons should use nocturne backgrounds with light text; destructive states should shift to oxblood. Inputs should feel like writing surfaces, not SaaS forms, with comfortable padding and strong focus treatment.

### Iconography & Imagery
Use a thin-stroke outline icon set such as **Lucide** as the baseline. Icons should support clarity, not perform the atmosphere by themselves. If bespoke decorative marks are added, keep them sparse and typographic. Imagery should focus on product surfaces, archival crops, and texture details rather than stock photography or fantasy character art. Avoid bats, dripping blood, and anything that reads like themed packaging.

### Accessibility Commitments
Target WCAG 2.1 AA across the product. Maintain at least 4.5:1 contrast for body text and 3:1 for large display text. Provide visible focus rings with at least 3px of apparent thickness using `--color-gold` over dark surfaces and `--color-nocturne` over light surfaces. Minimum touch target is 44px by 44px. Support full keyboard navigation for setup, archive, and recap flows. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` for all transitions. Ensure prompt and recap changes are announced to screen readers when route-level context changes.

### Motion & Interaction
Default transitions should live between 160ms and 240ms with `cubic-bezier(0.2, 0.8, 0.2, 1)`. Use motion for reveal, focus, and continuity, not delight for its own sake. Archive cards can fade and rise slightly on entry; memory loss or diary placement can use tonal transitions rather than dramatic animation. Loading states should prefer skeleton structure over spinners whenever the page context is already known.
