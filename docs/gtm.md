# Go-to-Market — Thousand Year Old Vampire: Digital Edition

## 1. Market Context

This product lives at the overlap of three niches that are individually small but collectively meaningful: solo journaling games, digitally native narrative players, and people who follow carefully authored indie software and game projects. The opportunity is not a mass-market horror game launch. It is a high-affinity release for people who care about literary interactive experiences but often need a friendlier medium than paper-first play. That makes the market modest in raw volume but unusually responsive to quality, taste, and trust.

Why now is straightforward. More players are comfortable treating a web app as a serious creative object, and build-in-public culture makes it easier to gather the right early audience before release. At the same time, many players live almost entirely on screens; if they are going to discover a journaling game, it is more likely to be through screenshots, devlogs, Discord threads, or a link from a friend than through a physical-store purchase. The one gating factor is legal posture: if rights for public adaptation are not settled, the GTM plan must bias toward private beta and craft-sharing rather than broad public distribution.

## 2. Launch Strategy

The launch should happen in three stages. **Pre-launch** is about building trust with the exact kind of player who responds to atmosphere, literary care, and thoughtful adaptation. That means public progress sharing focused on tone, interface choices, archive design, and onboarding lessons rather than generic “shipping features” posts. **Soft launch** should be a limited beta with invited players from solo RPG, narrative game, and adjacent literary-game communities. The goal is not scale. The goal is to learn whether the product actually preserves the feeling of the original once real people begin and return. **Public launch** should happen only after the onboarding path, memory/diary loop, and recap-return flow are stable and the legal/licensing posture is clear enough to support open sharing.

Do not try to stack too many channels. The product's credibility will come from taste and specificity, not reach. Launch like a well-made small thing, not an ambitious startup.

## 3. Pre-Launch Playbook

- **Week -8:** Publish a short positioning post explaining the project: what *Thousand Year Old Vampire* means as a solitary gothic experience, why digital adaptation is hard, and what this project is trying to preserve. Share on X, Bluesky, and one personal site or devlog. Goal: establish intent, not collect signups at scale.
- **Week -7:** Post one screenshot or short clip focused on the landing page and art direction, plus one short note about the difference between “removing friction” and “flattening the game.” Respond thoughtfully in solo-RPG and narrative-design threads where relevant; do not spam links into unrelated spaces.
- **Week -6:** Share the guided setup flow and talk about the magic-moment problem: getting the player into the fiction before the rules feel heavy. Invite 5–10 interested people to a private interest list using a simple waitlist form or email capture.
- **Week -5:** Post archive-focused updates: how memories, diary entries, or trait loss are being visualized. This is a good moment to engage in Discord communities, itch.io community spaces, or subreddit threads where people discuss journaling games and digital adaptations.
- **Week -4:** Recruit the first small beta wave. Target existing solo RPG fans, adjacent narrative players, and at least a couple of people who admire the genre but rarely play it. Ask for specific feedback on onboarding, prompt flow, and return-session clarity.
- **Week -3:** Publish a craft post with excerpts of lessons learned from beta without disclosing private user writing. Share concrete adjustments: what changed in setup, what felt too “tool-like,” what became more atmospheric.
- **Week -2:** Tighten the landing page and publish a “what to expect in the beta” post. Include device support, expected play style, and the fact that this is a solitary, writing-centered experience. This filters in the right users and filters out people looking for a different kind of game.
- **Week -1:** Confirm launch assets, update screenshots, prep launch copy, and privately re-test the full path with 3–5 players who were not in the first feedback round. If legal posture is still uncertain, convert “public launch” into a larger invite wave rather than a public blast.

## 4. Launch Week Plan

- **Day 1:** Publish the main launch post on X, Bluesky, and the project site. The post should lead with the emotional premise, then the product promise, then the link. Be available for replies for a few concentrated windows rather than being online all day.
- **Day 2:** Share one focused follow-up post about the archive and memory-loss design. This keeps the conversation from collapsing into “here is another indie web app” framing.
- **Day 3:** Post in carefully chosen communities where self-promotion is allowed: solo RPG Discords, narrative game forums, itch.io devlogs, and any newsletter or community thread where reflective games are discussed. Adapt the copy for each community instead of pasting one generic pitch.
- **Day 4:** Publish a short thread or post titled something like “What digital adaptation had to preserve to feel like the original.” This reinforces intent and answers skepticism from existing fans.
- **Day 5:** Send personal outreach to a small set of reviewers, designers, or community curators who care about literary or solo games. Keep it short: why they might care, what makes the adaptation different, and why now is a good time to look.
- **Day 6:** Review analytics and feedback. Look specifically at sign-up completion, setup completion, first prompt resolution, and second-session return. Do not chase vanity traffic.
- **Day 7:** Publish a quiet “first week reflections” note. Mention one thing players responded to, one thing that still needs work, and one upcoming adjustment. This keeps the build-in-public loop alive after the launch spike.

## 5. Post-Launch Growth

For weeks 1–4, focus almost entirely on product learning. Interview returning users, collect beta feedback, and watch recordings or notes from failed onboarding attempts if people consent. The main question is whether the product is genuinely inviting new people into the genre or only serving existing fans more conveniently.

For weeks 5–8, double down on whatever message is actually working. If archive screenshots drive curiosity, make more content around the chronicle object. If setup-flow posts convert better, focus on the ritual of becoming undead and the design lessons there. This is also the moment to refine the landing page based on actual objections.

For weeks 9–12, decide whether the project is staying a free artistic release, becoming a more formal public beta, or moving toward a licensed/official path. Growth at this stage should still be intimacy-driven, not scale-driven. The wrong audience will create more work than value.

## 6. Channel Strategy

**1. Build-in-public posts on X and Bluesky**  
Expected effort is low to medium and the return can be high because the founder's own taste and voice are part of the product story. Post two or three times per week: one product surface, one design lesson, one reflection on adaptation or play. Results should appear within days if the posts are specific.

**2. Solo RPG and narrative-game communities**  
This is the highest-trust channel, but it requires care. Expected effort is medium because community participation must be genuine. The return is slower but higher quality. Show up to discuss design and player experience, not just to drop links.

**3. Personal outreach to curators, reviewers, and designers**  
Expected effort is medium because each message should be tailored, but the audience size is small and manageable. The return is reputational rather than volume-based: one thoughtful endorsement from the right person matters more than broad shallow traffic.

**4. itch.io devlog / project page**  
Expected effort is medium. Return is moderate, but the audience is aligned with indie and experimental digital work. This is especially valuable if the project remains a free artistic release for a while.

**5. Product Hunt or broader launch platforms**  
Expected effort is medium to high and likely lower ROI for this specific project. Use only if the landing page, explanation, and legal posture are strong. Otherwise it risks attracting the wrong users.

## 7. Content Strategy

The content should orbit three themes. First, **ritual design**: how to get a player into the fiction before the rules feel heavy. Second, **archive design**: how memory, loss, and continuity are shown in the interface. Third, **adaptation discipline**: what had to be preserved for the digital version to still feel literary and solitary.

Publish two to three short-form posts each week on social channels and one longer reflection every two weeks on a devlog, personal site, or itch.io page. Use screenshots, cropped archive details, motion snippets, and short prose excerpts from UI copy. Avoid generic dev screenshots of schema diagrams or deployment logs unless they directly support the story.

## 8. Community Strategy

Show up where the audience already gathers instead of trying to build a standalone community too early. That likely means solo RPG Discord servers, indie narrative game circles, itch.io community spaces, small subreddits where journaling games are discussed, and feeds of designers who care about quiet interactive work.

The right stance is contribution-first. Talk about adaptation, interface tone, writing friction, and return-session continuity. Ask precise questions. Share learnings. Invite beta participation only after you have already demonstrated that the project has a point of view and is worth the audience's attention.

Do not spin up a dedicated Discord in the earliest stage unless there is clear demand. Small projects often mistake “I made a server” for community formation.

## 9. Key Metrics

**Acquisition:** 300 waitlist or interested-click visitors before launch, with at least 50 actual sign-ins in the first two weeks after public availability. More important than raw traffic is source quality: at least half of sign-ins should come from narrative-game or craft-focused channels rather than broad dev audiences.

**Activation:** 50 percent of authenticated users should create a chronicle, and at least 35 percent should complete setup and resolve their first prompt. If first-prompt completion drops much lower, the product is not delivering the core promise.

**Retention:** At least 8 users should return for a second session within 14 days in the first 90-day window, matching the product vision's success threshold. A stronger signal is 15 or more returns with meaningful archive usage.

**Revenue:** `$0` by design for the initial release. The key revenue-adjacent metric is qualitative: enough engagement and goodwill to justify a future decision about staying free, seeking licensing, or exploring monetization.

## 10. Budget Considerations

This launch can stay lean. Hosting and analytics can remain on free tiers initially. The real budget spend is likely to be on domain, visual asset polish, and possibly licensing or legal review if the project moves toward broader distribution. Keep hard monthly software spend below `$30` until the product proves that people return to it.

The scarcest resource is time and attention. That means budget should bias toward tools that reduce friction in release and feedback loops, not toward paid acquisition. If money is spent at all early, spend it on the product's credibility: domain, screenshots, polish, and rights clarity.

## 11. Risks

The biggest GTM risk is **rights ambiguity**. If adaptation rights are unclear, a broad public launch creates unnecessary exposure. Mitigation: run a private beta or explicitly limited release while clarifying legal posture.

The second risk is **audience mismatch from build-in-public channels**. Developer audiences may appreciate the craft but never become real users. Mitigation: write about atmosphere, narrative design, and player experience rather than generic shipping updates.

Another risk is **overpromising fidelity**. Existing fans may be skeptical of digital adaptation. Mitigation: show the product's restraint and explain what was preserved, not just what was streamlined.

There is also **channel fatigue** risk. A niche project can feel repetitive if every post says “still working on it.” Mitigation: rotate between interface snapshots, design lessons, adaptation insights, and player feedback reflections.

Finally, there is **premature scale** risk. If broader channels send a large but poorly matched audience before onboarding is stable, the product will collect noise instead of insight. Mitigation: treat soft launch as a real stage, not a symbolic one.
